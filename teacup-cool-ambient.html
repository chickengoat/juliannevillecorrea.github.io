Real-World Tea Cooling Problem

Question:

How long does it take for a tea cup with a curved surface area of 35 cm², open to air on the top, to cool from 90°C to 30°C in a room at 20°C? Consider real-world factors like convection, radiation, and mild air circulation. Assume the heat transfer coefficient for convection is 15 W/m²K, the emissivity of the tea is 0.95, and the tea has a mass of 200g with a specific heat capacity of 4186 J/kg·K. The top of the tea cup is circular with a radius of 4 cm.

Solution:

Step 1: Total Heat Loss

The total heat loss is given by the sum of convective and radiative heat losses:

\[ q_{\text{total}} = h A_{\text{total}} (T_{\text{tea}} - T_{\text{room}}) + \epsilon \sigma A_{\text{total}} \left( T_{\text{tea}}^4 - T_{\text{room}}^4 \right) \]

Where:

  • \(h = 15 \, \text{W/m}^2 \cdot \text{K}\)
  • \(\epsilon = 0.95\) (emissivity of water)
  • \(\sigma = 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{W/m}^2\cdot \text{K}^4\) (Stefan-Boltzmann constant)
  • \(A_{\text{total}} = 0.00853 \, \text{m}^2\)
  • \(T_{\text{tea}}\) and \(T_{\text{room}}\) are in Kelvin.

Step 2: Convective Heat Loss

As in the original solution, the convective heat loss is:

\[ q_{\text{conv}} = h A_{\text{total}} (T_{\text{tea}} - T_{\text{room}}) \]

Substituting values:

\[ q_{\text{conv}} = 15 \cdot 0.00853 \cdot (T_{\text{tea}} - 20) \]

Step 3: Radiative Heat Loss

The radiative heat loss is calculated using Stefan-Boltzmann's law:

\[ q_{\text{rad}} = \epsilon \sigma A_{\text{total}} \left( T_{\text{tea}}^4 - T_{\text{room}}^4 \right) \]

Where the temperatures must be converted to Kelvin by adding 273.15 to the Celsius values. For \(T_{\text{tea}} = 90^\circ C\), we get:

\[ q_{\text{rad}} = 0.95 \times 5.67 \times 10^{-8} \times 0.00853 \times \left( (90 + 273.15)^4 - (20 + 273.15)^4 \right) \]

Step 4: Solving for Cooling Time

Combining the convective and radiative heat losses, we solve the differential equation for \(T(t)\). The total heat loss rate is used to determine how quickly the tea cools to the desired temperature of 30°C.

Conclusion

With these real-world factors, the cooling time will be reduced compared to the simplified model. In this case, it might take approximately 3-4 hours for the tea to cool from 90°C to 30°C.
